Snowy Daze to Snowy Days: A Metaphor for Reclaiming Intrinsic Value

November 20th, 2019

From an early age, I watched adults quantify their children’s success relative to others, constantly comparing them to their friends’ children’s achievement. “Jimmy knows how to add?? Well, Susan learned her times tables up to seven!”

Impressionable children as we were, I know that some of us internalized the sentiment that our self-worth was reflective of our achievements in relation to others. We traversed the bell curve of life in our heavy-set glossed-marble sleds, numbing both our fingers and mind to the frigid sleet that laced our path. Frozen in the gravity of it all, we were left no other choice but to continue to jettison in our paralyzed abandon--willing our momentum to clear just one more standard deviation—if nothing at all, just one more standard deviation.

And maybe it worked for a while. Mom and Dad would pull our sled up the inclined terrain at first with their words of motivation. “You’re doing great honey!” “We’re right here with you!” And we smiled. We thought to ourselves, that maybe in the end of it, we could build snowmen out of the broken twigs left behind from our morning’s sled-ride. We lulled ourselves with the fantasies of a hill well-conquered and the last few moments of daybreak, taking in the magic of it all, embracing the earth and pine, and waiting for the last snowflakes to melt onto our plump, rosy cheeks.

But as we grew older, those hours of unadulterated happiness became minutes. And those minutes, eventually fleeting moments. That’s the trouble with extrinsic motivation and viewing self-worth through a lens of success. The precursor of it is innocuous, pleasant and fun, really. But the moment we calculate the extent of our character and intrinsic value with material measurements or in comparison to others, is the moment we allow ourselves to spiral down a path that eventually blinds us from our intrinsic merit--the human sentiments that color our lives and the people around us. We numb ourselves to a mindset of destitution, and the frostbite of our complacency leaves the nerves endings in our fingers lost and mind permanently altered.

The sleet-paved path seems intoxicatingly alluring, yes. There’s this indescribable rush of gliding through a glacial lustre of finish lines erected by the expectations of others. But feelings of inadequacy can be immobilizing, and an insurmountable slope freezes us in a stupor of self-perceived failure and blunder. So let’s take a moment to toss our sleek, polished sleds to the side. Let’s sink into the respite of our unfettered abandon, waving our arms and legs in the flurry of its wreckage. We’ll leave dirt-caked snow angels in our wake, powdered sun-burnt noses perched toward the sky, and brash, yellow-stained smiles purer than next winter’s snow.

Nostalgia

March 7, 2018

When I was ten
I would bore tunnels into tightly woven winter sweaters
I’d guide my chubby finger tips down arachne’s masterfully knitted patchworks
Twirling them between her cross-thatched stitching,
I turned winter’s sweater sleeves into fingerless gloves
I pressed the tattered fleece against my lips,
And prayed it would never lose its firmly stitched naïveté

When I was twelve
I’d wake up Christmas morning to slip those woolen sweaters on
Sliding monkey-bar blistered fingers through the holes its sleeves bore and opened the windowsill,
I outstretched arms toward the sky to catch snowflakes on soft rosy fingertips
I pressed the half-melted snowflakes against my lips
And prayed they would never melt

When I was sixteen
I would walk through cobble paved suburban corridors and houses clad with white picket fence armour
And pluck firebrick pomegranates from my neighbors’ overgrown gardens
Jabbing my fingers into a midday harvest treasure trove
I revealed the most regal crimson rubies
I pressed the fire lustered gems against my lips
And prayed they would never stop gleaming crimson red

And when I was eighteen
I would think wistfully to the memories I had made in my childhood
Praying and praying
That time would slow
If only for a moment


Life of Pets: A Personal Narrative About an Unlikely Hero

April 18, 2019

My time spent as a volunteer at a local hospital has taught me many things. It has taught me that when Agatha asks for vanilla bean ice cream on her lunch tray, she means vanilla bean, and NOT vanilla. It has taught me to treasure the mundaneness of tedious work; community service and humanitarianism isn’t always creating world peace and ending world hunger. But it has also taught me a few bleak realities pervading nearly every hospital.

The truth of the matter is that hospital ambiances are incomprehensibly lonely, oftentimes sucking the life out of individuals who are fighting for their lives. In the rehab department, which is predominantly composed elderly folk, some patients rarely receive human interaction or visits from their loved ones, except for the occasional visit of a blasé vital sign monitor. As nurses consult each other, patients are referred to as “Room 148, Bed 2.” Individuals are confined to plain white rooms behind “contact precautions” and constant supervision. While it is true that healthcare is making marvelous advances in healthcare innovations, treatments, and emphasis on patient outcomes, inescapable emotional sterility still pervades.

To me, the niché for the humanitarian pursuit of “patient wellbeing” persists as a gapingly unaddressed vacancy. And that’s when I saw them. Maxx the husky’s piercing eyes and Buxton the Anatolian shepherd’s majestic coat as they trudged along the hospital halls, mouths agape and slobbering in all their glory. Maxx and Buxton never ceased to lighten the mood, but more than anything, they acted as a bridge for patients, visitors, and nurses alike, creating genuine conversations and visceral human interaction. The therapeutic effect these gumpy pupperz had was unmistakable, leaving patients with smiles and reminders of the very feeling they are healing for. I can’t express how incredibly humbling it was to see these *slightly* smelly dogs initiate such a meaningful change in patients’ demeanor, if only for a second. Patients opened up and talked about their own dog with a nostalgic gleam in their eyes. They reveled in the sense of comfort they get from contact with these dogs, therapeutic as it was. It was a bittersweet feeling for me–sweet in that these dogs could uplift these individuals, but bitter in that they were in a position where they necessitated uplifting in the first place. I believe that as members of GlobeMed, we can empathize with this feeling all to well. We of course feel disheartened as we observe inequities and individuals put into these unfair situations that detract from their well-being. But we can revel in that fact that we are a part of a mechanism that empowers these very people, GlobeMed being synonymous with my two adorable pooch friends in the analogy, of course.

Buxton and Maxx, wise sages as they are, have quite a few lessons to teach us. They teach us that working towards emotional fulfillment and visceral human interaction should never be on the back-burner of our lives. They remind us to always be cognizant and mindful of our own emotional wellbeing and the emotional wellbeing of others; we never know when a person might need that extra surge of support and comfort to reinvigorate them to move forward. And most importantly, they remind us, cliched as it is, that living transcends the physical state of being.

Mancala with AI Player Component

C++, Linux


Mancala is a two-player turn-based strategy game in which the objective is to capture the majority of your opponent's beans on the board. Leveraging C++ and Linux for testing, I utilized object-oriented programming to create a fully functionable game of mancala, adhering to the intricacies and rules of this complex game, while displaying the progress of the game on the IDE. This program has two modes: the first being player versus player and the second being player versus CPU. Utilizing recursion in conjunction with game trees, I implemented varying difficulty levels for prospective mancala lovers to play against. Most notably, my minimax function calculates the most optimal move based on a designated depth value, returning the move that will yield the greatest advantage based on recursive evaluations of its effect several turns in advance.

  • Date: 2019
  • Categories: Object-Oriented Programming, AI

C++, Linux

In the process of designing this algorithm, I considered the tradeoffs of time complexity and space complexity, architecting a program that kept these two imperative considerations in mind.
